## Origin

The shika radical is a detailed pictograph of a deer. The top part represents the antlers, the middle section is the head and body, and the bottom strokes represent the four legs. It captures the graceful and majestic form of this forest creature.

## Story

Prance through the forest! Imagine a magnificent stag with branching antlers peering through the trees. This radical represents grace, nature, and the festive elegance of wildlife. When you see it, think of the deep woods, the beauty of the natural world, and the "royal" presence of a deer in the landscape.

## Position

Often appears as a standalone kanji or as the top/outer component of a character.

## Transforms Meaning

Indicates deer, wildlife, forest beauty, elegance, or concepts related to jubilation and majestic appearances.

## Example Kanji

### 鹿 (deer)

The base kanji for a deer, used in geography (like Kagoshima or Nara) and to describe the animal itself.

### 麗 (beautiful / lovely)

Two antlers (variants) + deer (鹿). Represents the extreme "beauty" and elegance of two majestic deer standing together—the ultimate symbol of loveliness.

### 慶 (jubilation / celebrate)

Deer (鹿 variant) + heart + movement. Originally related to a "celebration" where a deer was presented as a gift—now used for great "jubilation" and joy.
